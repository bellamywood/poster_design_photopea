Step 03 — Canvas & Background
SET UP & PLACE BACKGROUND
1

Create Your Canvas

In Canva, click Create a design → search for Poster and select A4 Portrait (or choose Custom size and enter 594mm × 841mm for A4). This gives you a professional portrait canvas. Name your design at the top — something like My Movie Poster.

2

Upload and Place Your Background

Click the Uploads tab in the left panel → Upload files → select your background image. Once uploaded, click it or drag it onto the canvas. To make it fill the whole canvas, drag the corner handles out to the edges — you'll see purple guide lines appear when it snaps. Right-click → Set as background to lock it in place behind everything else.

Watch — Setting Up Canva and Placing Your Background

Canva saves automatically. Check the top bar shows "All changes saved" before moving on.

03
Step 03 — Hero Character
PLACE & CUT YOUR HERO
3

Upload and Place Your Main Subject

In the Uploads tab, upload your hero character or main subject. Click it to add it to the canvas. Resize by dragging the corner handles — hold Shift to keep proportions. Drag it to where you want it to sit in the composition. It will automatically appear as a new layer above the background.

4

Remove the Background from Your Subject

Select your hero image. In the toolbar at the top, click Edit image → then click BG Remover. Canva will automatically remove the background. If it makes any mistakes, use the Erase and Restore brushes to tidy up the edges. Click Apply when you're happy.

Watch — Removing the Background from Your Hero in Canva
🪜 BG Remover Not Working Well?
  • The BG Remover works best when the subject is clearly separate from the background in colour
  • Use the Erase brush (in BG Remover) to remove any leftover background patches
  • Use the Restore brush to bring back any parts of your subject that got removed by accident
  • Adjust the brush size using the slider for precise edits around edges

Canva saves automatically. Check the top bar shows "All changes saved" before moving on.

Add Props & Create Depth

Step 04
HERO SHOT
ANATOMY ELEMENT 1 — MAIN IMAGE / HERO SHOT
The Hero Shot is the main image of your poster — it establishes character, tone, and story at a glance. In professional movie posters the hero is always positioned with purpose: where they sit in the frame, how large they are, what they face — all of this communicates meaning before a single word is read.
Key Objectives
ALL Position your hero character so they are clearly the main focus of the poster
MOST Use scale and placement to create drama — consider what the hero faces or where they look
SOME Apply a colour adjustment to your hero so they feel part of the scene
Watch — Composing Your Hero Shot in Canva
04
Step 04 — Techniques
COMPOSING YOUR HERO
1

Position for Drama

Click and drag your hero to reposition them. Try placing them slightly off-centre — this is more cinematic than dead centre. Use the corner handles to resize. Canva's purple guide lines will help you align elements. Think about what the hero faces or where they look — this tells a story without words.

2

Match the Lighting with Image Adjustments

Select your hero image → click Edit image in the top toolbar → go to Adjust. Use the Tint slider to shift the colour of your subject towards the overall tone of the background. Lower the Saturation slightly (around −10 to −20) for a more cinematic, filmic feel.

3

Add a Drop Shadow (Extension)

Select your hero → click Edit image → Shadows. Try the Drop shadow effect, reduce the blur and adjust the angle so light appears to come from above or to the side. This grounds your character in the scene and adds depth.

★ Extension — Rule of Thirds
  • In Canva: View → Show rulers and guides. The guide lines help you divide the canvas into thirds. Place your hero's eyes or face at one of the four intersection points — this is the professional rule of thirds.
  • You can also drag guide lines manually from the ruler edges.

TITLE & TYPOGRAPHY
ANATOMY ELEMENT 2 — TITLE / TYPOGRAPHY
The font you choose signals the genre before anyone reads the words. A scratchy, cracked font says horror. A bold, blocky font says action. Clean metallic text says sci-fi. Rounded, playful letters say comedy or family. Your title needs to be readable from a distance and immediately communicate the mood of the film.
Key Objectives
ALL Add the movie title in a font that suits your genre
MOST Adjust font size, colour, and spacing so the title dominates the poster
SOME Apply a text effect (shadow, glow, or lift) to elevate the title
Sci-Fi
Impact · Bebas · Orbitron
Bold, geometric, metallic
Comedy
Rounded · Chunky · Fun
Warm, playful, approachable
Horror
Serif · Distressed · Thin
Unsettling, sharp, tense

05
Step 05 — Techniques
STYLING YOUR TITLE
1

Add the Title Text

Click Text in the left panel → click Add a heading. A text box appears — type your movie title in all capitals for impact. To change the font, click the font name in the top toolbar and search for a genre-appropriate font. Canva has thousands — try Impact, Bebas Neue, Pacifico, or search by style.

2

Size, Colour, and Position

Make the title the largest text on the poster — drag the corners to resize, or type a large number into the font size box in the toolbar. Change the colour by clicking the colour swatch in the toolbar and choosing from the colour picker or entering a hex code. Place the title in the lower third of the poster — a classic theatrical convention.

3

Add a Text Effect

Select the title text box → click Effects in the top toolbar. Choose an effect that fits your genre: Shadow for drama and depth, Glow for sci-fi or fantasy, Lift for a bold 3D look, Neon for an electric feel. Adjust the intensity slider to keep it looking professional rather than overdone.

🪜 Can't Find a Good Font?
  • In the font search bar, type keywords like "bold", "condensed", "rounded", or "serif" to filter results
  • Canva labels some fonts as Brand fonts — any unlabelled fonts are free to use
  • Match the font style to your genre — don't use a playful, rounded font for a thriller

Step 06
TAGLINE
ANATOMY ELEMENT 3 — TAGLINE
The tagline is a short, punchy phrase that hooks the audience. It creates mystery, excitement, or a sense of stakes. Your tagline should have been given to you in your scenario brief — it creates an immediate feeling in the viewer's mind and makes them want to see the film.
Key Objectives
ALL Add the tagline in a smaller font, clearly positioned near the title
MOST Style the tagline differently to the title — lighter weight, italic, or different colour
SOME Adjust letter spacing and opacity to give the tagline a professional, subtle quality
Watch — Adding Your Tagline in Canva
06
Step 06 — Techniques
STYLING YOUR TAGLINE
1

Add the Tagline Text

Click Text → Add a subheading. Type your tagline from your scenario brief. Choose a lighter, thinner font than your title — try a Light or Italic weight of your title font, or a different font entirely. The tagline should feel cinematic and understated next to the bold title.

2

Size, Position, and Alignment

Make the tagline noticeably smaller — roughly a third of the title's size. Position it above the title or between the hero image and the title. Use the alignment tool in the top toolbar to centre the text on the canvas for a professional look.

3

Adjust Spacing and Opacity

With the tagline text selected, click Spacing in the top toolbar and increase Letter spacing to around 5–8 for a cinematic, wide-set look. Then click the Transparency icon (the checkerboard square in the toolbar) and reduce opacity to around 75–85% so it sits subtly behind the title.

COLOUR PALETTE
ANATOMY ELEMENT 4 — COLOUR PALETTE
Colour communicates genre instantly. Dark blues and greens suggest thriller and danger. Warm yellows and oranges suggest comedy and warmth. Washed-out tones suggest dystopia. Bright, saturated primaries suggest family adventure. Look at your genre and choose colours that immediately signal the right mood to your audience.
Genre Colour Examples
Sci-Fi — Deep Blue
Comedy — Warm Gold
Horror — Blood Red
Action — Dark Green
Key Objectives
ALL Use image adjustments to shift your background towards your genre's colour range
MOST Apply a colour overlay or tinted shape to unify the mood across the whole poster
SOME Use Canva's filters on multiple layers to create a consistent, cinematic colour grade
Watch — Applying Your Colour Palette in Canva
07
Step 07 — Techniques
COLOUR & ATMOSPHERE
1

Adjust the Background Colour

Click your background image → Edit image → Adjust. Use the Brightness, Contrast, and Saturation sliders to get the right feel. Reduce saturation slightly for a more cinematic, desaturated look. Use the Tint slider to shift the image towards your genre's dominant colour.

2

Apply a Colour Overlay for Atmosphere

Add a rectangle that covers the whole canvas: click Elements → search "rectangle" → add one and resize it to fill the canvas. Set its colour to your genre's dominant shade. Then click the Transparency icon and reduce opacity to 15–30%. This adds a colour wash that unifies the whole poster. Use layer ordering to place it above the background but below your subject and text.

3

Apply a Cinematic Filter (Extension)

Click your background image → Edit image → Filters. Browse the filter presets — options like Film, Fade, Gritty, or Studio can add a professional, filmic look. Adjust the intensity slider so it enhances without overwhelming.

★ Extension — Vignette Effect
  • Add an Ellipse shape from the Elements panel. Size it larger than the canvas and centre it. Colour it black. Reduce opacity to around 40–50%. This darkens the edges like a cinema lens — it's called a vignette, and it draws the eye to the centre of the poster.

Step 08
BILLING BLOCK
ANATOMY ELEMENT 5 — BILLING BLOCK
The billing block is the small text at the bottom of every professional movie poster — the names of actors, the director, the production company, and the film rating. It's tiny and often barely readable, but it signals that this is a real, professionally produced film. You need one on your poster to make it look authentic.
Key Objectives
ALL Add small credits text at the bottom of the poster — actors, director, studio, rating
MOST Style it in a narrow, condensed font at a small size so it looks authentic
SOME Include a film rating box (e.g. 12A, 15, PG) styled appropriately
08 / 09
Steps 08 & 09 — Techniques
BILLING BLOCK & STUDIO LOGO
ANATOMY ELEMENTS 5 & 6
1

Add the Billing Block Text

Click Text → Add a little bit of body text. A small text box appears — type your mock credits: e.g. [ACTOR NAME] · [ACTOR NAME] · A TORCH FILMS PRODUCTION · Screenplay by [Your Name] · Directed by [Your Name] · Music by [Your Name] · © 2025 · Rated [U / PG / 12A]. Stretch the text box to span the full width of the poster.

2

Style It Correctly

Select a condensed or narrow font — search for "condensed" or "narrow" in the font panel. Set the size to around 6–9pt (very small). Set the colour to white or light grey, depending on your background. Click the Transparency icon and reduce opacity to 70–80% — this is intentionally hard to read. That's how real billing blocks look.

3

Add the Studio Logo

Click Uploads → upload your studio logo PNG. Click it to add it to the canvas. Place it in the bottom-left or bottom-right corner — roughly the same height as your billing block text. If the logo has a dark background that shouldn't be there, use Edit image → BG Remover to clean it up. Reduce opacity to around 80% so it blends naturally.

4

Add a Film Rating Box (Extension)

Click Elements → add a small rectangle. Fill it with white or a bold colour. Add a text layer on top with your rating (e.g. U, PG, 12A) in a bold font. Group the rectangle and text together (select both → right-click → Group) and position the rating in the bottom right corner.

🪜 Where to Find Studio Logos
  • Check the pre-prepared assets folder — logos are already saved as transparent PNGs
  • Or search Google: "[Studio name] logo PNG transparent" — filter by Creative Commons
  • If the logo has a white background, use Canva's BG Remover to make it transparent
