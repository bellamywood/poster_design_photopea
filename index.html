Step 03 — Canvas & Background
SET UP & PLACE BACKGROUND
1

Set Up Your Canvas

File → New → click the Print tab → select A4. Name it something like my_movie_poster. Click Create.

2

Place Your Background

Go to File → Open and Place → select your background image. Once placed, make sure Transform Controls are enabled — with the Move tool selected, tick Transform Controls in the top toolbar. Drag the corner handles to fill the entire canvas. Press Enter to confirm. In the Layers panel, drag this layer to the very bottom.

Step 03 — Hero Character
PLACE & CUT YOUR HERO
3

Add Your Main Subject

Go to File → Open and Place → select your hero character or main subject. Resize using the corner handles — do not hold Shift, as this will distort the proportions. Press Enter to confirm. This layer should sit above the background in the Layers panel.

4

Remove the Background from Your Subject

In the Layers panel, right-click your character layer → select Rasterize. Then select the Magic Wand tool from the left toolbar. Click on an area of the background you want to remove, then press Delete. Repeat for any remaining background areas. If you make a mistake, go to Edit → Undo (or press Ctrl+Z) to step back.

Watch — Removing the Background from Your Hero
🪜 Made a Mistake Removing the Background?
  • Press Ctrl+Z to undo the last step — you can press it multiple times to go further back
  • Or go to Edit → Undo from the top menu
  • Magic Wand works best when the background colour is clearly different from the subject — try adjusting the Tolerance value in the top toolbar (lower = more precise)

Step 04
HERO SHOT
ANATOMY ELEMENT 1 — MAIN IMAGE / HERO SHOT
The Hero Shot is the main image of your poster — it establishes character, tone, and story at a glance. In professional movie posters the hero is always positioned with purpose: where they sit in the frame, how large they are, what they face — all of this communicates meaning before a single word is read.
Key Objectives
ALL Position your hero character so they are clearly the main focus of the poster
MOST Use scale and placement to create drama — consider what the hero faces or where they look
SOME Apply a colour or light adjustment to your hero so they feel part of the scene
Watch — Composing Your Hero Shot
04
Step 04 — Techniques
COMPOSING YOUR HERO
1

Position for Drama

Move your hero slightly off-centre — this is more cinematic than dead centre. Think about what they face or where they look. Use the Move tool and Free Transform (Ctrl+Alt+T) to adjust size and position.

2

Match the Lighting

Your hero should feel like they belong in the scene. Select the hero layer → Image → Adjustments → Hue/Saturation. Shift the hue slightly to match the overall colour tone of your background. Reduce saturation by 10–15 to feel more cinematic.

3

Add a Subtle Rim Light (Extension)

Duplicate your hero layer. Filter → Blur → Gaussian Blur (set to 8px). Change the blend mode to Screen and reduce opacity to 40%. This creates a glow effect that suggests light hitting the edges of the character.

★ Extension — Rule of Thirds
  • In Photopea: View → Show → Grid. The grid divides your canvas into thirds. Place your hero's eyes or face at one of the four intersection points — this is the professional rule of thirds.
  • Turn the grid off when you're happy: View → Show → Grid again.

TITLE & TYPOGRAPHY
ANATOMY ELEMENT 2 — TITLE / TYPOGRAPHY
The font you choose signals the genre before anyone reads the words. A scratchy, cracked font says horror. A bold, blocky font says action. Clean metallic text says sci-fi. Rounded, playful letters say comedy or family. Your title needs to be readable from a distance and immediately communicate the mood of the film.
Key Objectives
ALL Add the movie title in a font that suits your genre
MOST Adjust font size, colour, and spacing so the title dominates the poster
SOME Apply a layer style effect (glow, bevel, or gradient overlay) to elevate the title
Sci-Fi
IMPACT · BEBAS · ORBITRON
Bold, geometric, metallic
Comedy
Rounded · Chunky · Fun
Warm, playful, approachable
Horror
Serif · Distressed · Thin
Unsettling, sharp, tense

Step 05 — Techniques
STYLING YOUR TITLE
1

Add the Title Text

Select the Text tool (T) from the toolbar. Click on your canvas. Type your movie title in all capitals for impact. Select the text and choose a font that matches your genre — use the font dropdown in the toolbar and type keywords like "bold", "rounded", or "condensed" to filter options.

2

Size and Position

The title should be the largest text on the poster. Use Ctrl+Alt+T (Free Transform) to resize. Place it in the lower third of the poster — this is a common professional convention for theatrical posters.

3

Add a Layer Style Effect

Double-click the title layer in the Layers panel. This opens Layer Style. Try: Outer Glow (set colour to match your palette, opacity 60%) or Gradient Overlay (two colours from your palette, angle 90°). This gives your title a polished, professional finish.

🪜 Can't Find a Good Font?
  • In the Text tool, click the font dropdown and type keywords to filter choices
  • Photopea includes Google Fonts — great options include Bebas Neue, Anton, Russo One, Pacifico, and Cinzel
  • Match the font style to your genre — don't use a playful font for a thriller

TAGLINE
ANATOMY ELEMENT 3 — TAGLINE
The tagline is a short, punchy phrase that hooks the audience. It creates mystery, excitement, or a sense of stakes. Your tagline should have been given to you in your scenario brief — it creates an immediate feeling in the viewer's mind and makes them want to see the film.
Key Objectives
ALL Add the tagline in a smaller font, clearly positioned near the title
MOST Style the tagline differently to the title — lighter weight, italic, or different colour
SOME Adjust letter spacing and opacity to give the tagline a professional, subtle quality
Watch — Adding Your Tagline
06
Step 06 — Techniques
STYLING YOUR TAGLINE
1

Add the Tagline Text

Select the Text tool. Click near the title. Type your tagline from your scenario brief. Use a lighter, thinner font than the title — try DM Sans Light, Lato Light, or Raleway in italic. The tagline should feel cinematic and understated.

2

Size and Position

The tagline should sit above the title or between the hero and the title. It should be noticeably smaller — about a third of the title's size. Centre-align the text for a professional look.

3

Adjust Spacing and Opacity

In the Character panel (Window → Character), increase the letter spacing (tracking) to 150–200 for a cinematic feel. You can also reduce the layer opacity to 80–85% so it sits behind the title visually.

COLOUR PALETTE
ANATOMY ELEMENT 4 — COLOUR PALETTE
Colour communicates genre instantly. Dark blues and greens suggest thriller and danger. Warm yellows and oranges suggest comedy and warmth. Washed-out tones suggest dystopia. Bright, saturated primaries suggest family adventure. Look at your genre and choose colours that immediately signal the right mood to your audience.
Genre Colour Examples
Sci-Fi — Deep Blue
Comedy — Warm Gold
Horror — Blood Red
Action — Dark Green
Key Objectives
ALL Use Hue/Saturation to shift your background towards your genre's colour range
MOST Apply a blend mode to create atmospheric lighting across multiple layers
SOME Add a gradient overlay or colour grade layer for a cinematic, unified look
Watch — Applying Your Colour Palette
07
Step 07 — Techniques
COLOUR & ATMOSPHERE
1

Adjust the Background Colour

Select your background layer → Image → Adjustments → Hue/Saturation. Shift the Hue slider towards the dominant colour of your genre palette. Adjust Lightness to darken or brighten the scene. Reduce Saturation slightly for a more cinematic, professional feel.

2

Apply a Blend Mode for Atmosphere

Create a new empty layer above the background. Use the Gradient tool: set colours from your dominant shade (top) to transparent (bottom). In the Layers panel, change the blend mode to Overlay or Screen at 40–60% opacity. This adds atmospheric depth that unifies the whole poster.

3

Add a Vignette (Extension)

Create a new layer at the very top. Use the Elliptical Marquee tool — drag a large oval selection in the centre, then go Select → Inverse. Fill with black (Edit → Fill → Black). Set layer opacity to 50% and blend mode to Multiply. This darkens the edges like a cinema lens.

★ Extension — Curves Colour Grade
  • Layer → New Adjustment Layer → Curves. Select individual colour channels (Red, Green, Blue) and make subtle adjustments. Pulling Blue up gives a cold, futuristic feel. Pulling Red up gives warmth. This is the same technique used in Hollywood colour grading.

BILLING BLOCK
ANATOMY ELEMENT 5 — BILLING BLOCK
The billing block is the small text at the bottom of every professional movie poster — the names of actors, the director, the production company, and the film rating. It's tiny and often barely readable, but it signals that this is a real, professionally produced film. You need one on your poster to make it look authentic.
Key Objectives
ALL Add small credits text at the bottom of the poster — actors, director, studio, rating
MOST Style it in a narrow, condensed font at a small size so it looks authentic
SOME Include a film rating box (e.g. 12A, 15, PG) styled appropriately
08 / 09
Steps 08 & 09 — Techniques
BILLING BLOCK & STUDIO LOGO
ANATOMY ELEMENTS 5 & 6
1

Add the Billing Block Text

Select the Text tool. Draw a wide text box across the bottom of the poster. Type mock credits — use names from your class or invent them: e.g. [ACTOR NAME] · [ACTOR NAME] · A TORCH FILMS PRODUCTION · Screenplay by [Your Name] · Directed by [Your Name] · Music by [Your Name] · © 2025 · Rated [U / PG / 12A]

2

Style It Correctly

Font: use a condensed/narrow font — try Arial Narrow or Barlow Condensed. Size: 6–8pt. Colour: white or light grey, or dark if your background is pale. Reduce layer opacity to 70–80%. This is intentionally hard to read — that's how real billing blocks look.

3

Add the Studio Logo

File → Place → select your studio logo PNG (it should have a transparent background). Place it in the bottom-left or bottom-right corner. Resize it to be small — roughly the same height as your billing block text. If the logo is dark, change its blend mode to Screen so it shows on a dark background. If it's on a light area, use Multiply.

4

Add a Film Rating Box (Extension)

Use the Rectangle tool to draw a small box. Fill it with white or a standard rating colour. Add a Text layer on top with your rating (e.g. U, PG, 12A) in a bold font. Group these layers and place the rating in the bottom right.

🪜 Where to Find Studio Logos
  • Check the assets folder — logos are already saved as transparent PNGs
  • Or search Google: "[Studio name] logo PNG transparent" — filter by Creative Commons
  • If the logo has a white background, use blend mode Screen to hide it
