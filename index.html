Step 04
HERO SHOT
ANATOMY ELEMENT 1 — MAIN IMAGE / HERO SHOT
The Hero Shot is the main image of your poster — it establishes character, tone, and story at a glance. In professional movie posters the hero is always positioned with purpose: where they sit in the frame, how large they are, what they face — all of this communicates meaning before a single word is read.
Key Objectives
ALL Position your hero character so they are clearly the main focus of the poster
MOST Use scale and placement to create drama — hero faces a threat, looks into the unknown
SOME Apply a colour or light adjustment to your hero so they feel part of the scene
Watch — Composing Your Hero Shot
~20–30 sec · PosterClip6_step6_heroshot.mp4
04
Step 04 — Techniques
COMPOSING YOUR HERO
1

Position for Drama

Move your hero slightly off-centre — this is more cinematic than dead centre. Try placing them lower in the frame so the threat or landscape looms above them. Use the Move tool and Free Transform (Ctrl+T) to adjust.

2

Match the Lighting

Your hero should feel like they belong in the scene. Select the hero layer → Image → Adjustments → Hue/Saturation. Shift the hue slightly towards blue/teal to match a cold space environment. Reduce saturation by 10–15 to feel more cinematic.

3

Add a Subtle Rim Light (Extension)

Duplicate your hero layer. Filter → Blur → Gaussian Blur (set to 8px). Change the blend mode to Screen and reduce opacity to 40%. This creates a glow effect that suggests light hitting the edges of the character.

★ Extension — Rule of Thirds
  • In Photopea: View → Show → Grid. The grid divides your canvas into thirds. Place your hero's eyes or face at one of the four intersection points — this is the professional rule of thirds.
  • Turn the grid off when you're happy: View → Show → Grid again.

Step 05
TITLE & TYPOGRAPHY
ANATOMY ELEMENT 2 — TITLE / TYPOGRAPHY
The font you choose signals the genre before anyone reads the words. A scratchy, cracked font says horror. A bold, blocky font says action. Clean metallic text says sci-fi thriller. Your title needs to be readable from a distance and immediately communicate the mood of the film.
Key Objectives
ALL Add the movie title in a font that suits the genre
MOST Adjust font size, colour, and spacing so the title dominates the poster
SOME Apply a layer style effect (glow, bevel, or gradient overlay) to elevate the title
Watch — Adding and Styling Your Title
~20–30 sec · PosterClip7_step7_title.mp4
05
Step 05 — Techniques
STYLING YOUR TITLE
1

Add the Title Text

Select the Text tool (T) from the toolbar. Click on your canvas. Type: GALACTIC RIFT. Use all capitals for impact. Select the text and change the font to something bold and geometric — try fonts like Impact, Bebas Neue, or Orbitron for sci-fi.

2

Size and Position

The title should be the largest text on the poster. Use Ctrl+T (Free Transform) to resize. Place it in the lower third of the poster — this is a common professional convention for theatrical posters.

3

Add a Layer Style Effect

Double-click the title layer in the Layers panel. This opens Layer Style. Try: Outer Glow (set colour to a cold blue/white, opacity 60%) or Gradient Overlay (white to light blue, angle 90°). This gives the metallic sci-fi feel.

🪜 Can't Find a Good Font?
  • In the Text tool, click the font dropdown and type "bold" to filter choices
  • Photopea includes Google Fonts — look for Bebas Neue, Anton, or Russo One
  • Avoid thin or handwriting fonts for this scenario — they won't read clearly

TAGLINE
ANATOMY ELEMENT 3 — TAGLINE
The tagline is a short, punchy phrase that hooks the audience. It creates mystery, excitement, or a sense of stakes. The tagline for Galactic Rift is: "A portal has opened. Not everyone will survive." — it creates immediate tension and raises a question in the viewer's mind.
Key Objectives
ALL Add the tagline in a smaller font, clearly positioned near the title
MOST Style the tagline differently to the title — lighter weight, italic, or different colour
SOME Adjust letter spacing and opacity to give the tagline a professional, subtle quality

Step 06 — Techniques
STYLING YOUR TAGLINE
1

Add the Tagline Text

Select the Text tool. Click near the title. Type: "A portal has opened. Not everyone will survive." Use a lighter, thinner font than the title — try DM Sans Light, Lato Light, or Raleway in italic.

2

Size and Position

The tagline should sit above the title or between the hero and the title. It should be noticeably smaller — about a third of the title's size. Centre-align the text for a professional look.

3

Adjust Spacing and Opacity

In the Character panel (Window → Character), increase the letter spacing (tracking) to 150–200 for a cinematic feel. You can also reduce the layer opacity to 80–85% so it sits behind the title visually.

Step 07 — Techniques
COLOUR & ATMOSPHERE
1

Adjust the Background Colour

Select your background layer → Image → Adjustments → Hue/Saturation. Shift Hue towards blue/purple. Reduce Lightness slightly to make the scene feel darker and more dramatic.

2

Apply a Blend Mode for Atmosphere

Create a new empty layer above the background. Use the Gradient tool: set colours from dark blue (top) to transparent (bottom). In the Layers panel change the blend mode to Overlay or Screen at 40–60% opacity. This adds atmospheric depth.

3

Add a Vignette (Extension)

Create a new layer at the very top. Use the Elliptical Marquee tool — drag a large oval selection in the centre, then go Select → Inverse. Fill with black (Edit → Fill → Black). Set layer opacity to 50% and blend mode to Multiply. This darkens the edges like a cinema lens.

★ Extension — Curves Colour Grade
  • Layer → New Adjustment Layer → Curves. Select the Blue channel and pull it slightly upward — this gives the whole poster a cold, futuristic colour cast used in real sci-fi films.

Step 08
BILLING BLOCK
ANATOMY ELEMENT 5 — BILLING BLOCK
The billing block is the small text at the bottom of every professional movie poster — the names of actors, the director, the production company, and the film rating. It's tiny and often barely readable, but it signals that this is a real, professionally produced film. You need one on your poster to make it look authentic.
Key Objectives
ALL Add small credits text at the bottom of the poster — actors, director, studio, rating
MOST Style it in a narrow, condensed font at a small size so it looks authentic
SOME Include a film rating box (e.g. 12A, 15, PG) styled appropriately

Steps 08 & 09 — Techniques
BILLING BLOCK & STUDIO LOGO
ANATOMY ELEMENTS 5 & 6
1

Add the Billing Block Text

Select the Text tool. Draw a wide text box across the bottom of the poster. Type mock credits — use names from your class or invent them: e.g. HARRISON SMITH · ELENA CROSS · MALIK JOHNSON · A TORCH FILMS PRODUCTION · Screenplay by [Your Name] · Directed by [Your Name] · © 2025

2

Style It Correctly

Font: use a condensed/narrow font — try Arial Narrow or Barlow Condensed. Size: 6–8pt. Colour: white or light grey. Reduce layer opacity to 70–80%. This is intentionally hard to read — that's how real billing blocks look.

3

Add the Studio Logo

File → Place → select your studio logo PNG (it should have a transparent background). Place it in the bottom-left or bottom-right corner. Resize it to be small — roughly the same height as your billing block text. If the logo is dark, change its blend mode to Screen so it shows on a dark background.

4

Add a Film Rating Box (Extension)

Use the Rectangle tool to draw a small box. Fill it with white or a standard rating colour. Add a Text layer on top: type 12A or 15 in a bold font. Group these layers and place the rating in the bottom right.

🪜 Where to Find Studio Logos
  • Check the assets folder — logos are already saved as transparent PNGs
  • Or search Google: "Warner Bros logo PNG transparent" — filter by Creative Commons
  • If the logo has a white background, use blend mode Screen to hide it

Boost the Background
